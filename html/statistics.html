Statistics

Motivation

Crime is a daily concern for many New Yorkers, whether long-time residents, newcomers, or tourists. Yet, finding clear, intuitive, and localized information about crime in the city is surprisingly difficult. Most existing tools are either too technical, outdated, or lacking context. Our website aims to change that.

We built an interactive platform that allows anyone, from a concerned resident to a curious visitor, to explore crimes committed around them with just a few clicks. Using a dynamic map and easy-to-use filters, users can see exactly what types of crimes have occurred in specific neighborhoods, and when. Whether you're walking through Midtown or living in the Bronx, the platform gives you direct visibility into your local safety landscape, something that’s often hidden in overwhelming datasets or hard-to-navigate public records.

But we didn’t stop at showing what happens. We also wanted to explore why. That’s why we integrated socio-economic data, like poverty and unemployment rates, into the experience. This added layer helps users understand the broader context: why some areas experience more crime than others, and how economic factors may influence safety. By combining intuitive crime mapping with meaningful social insight, our goal is to empower everyday users with knowledge. This tool isn't just for researchers or policymakers, it's for anyone who wants to better understand their city and make informed decisions, whether it's about where to live, travel, or advocate for change.

In this section, we will present some statistics that we have gathered from the data. We will also show you how to interact with the plots and what they mean. We hope that this will help you understand the data better and make it more accessible.

Crime Situation in New York City

Let's first take a look at the different types of crimes we considered. The dataset contained a wide variety of offenses, ranging from fraud and prostitution to kidnapping and murder. To simplify the analysis, we kept only the most common crime types and grouped them into categories that are easier for everyone to understand.

It is then interesting to examine which crime types are the most frequent in New York City. The plot below illustrates this by showing the percentage that each crime category represents among all reported incidents between 2006 and 2023. We observe that the majority of crimes involve theft or physical assault. Serious crimes like rape and murder, while less common, still represent a significant concern — with an annual average of 1,398 rapes, 411 murders, 1,085 arsons, and 186 kidnappings reported.

We can see that crime complaints are relatively balanced across the boroughs, except for Staten Island, which accounts for only 4.64% of reported crimes. This is largely explained by the fact that Staten Island makes up just 5.8% of New York City’s population. Overall, the distribution of complaints appears to correlate with population size. Boroughs with more residents tend to report more crimes. However, we cannot draw definitive conclusions from this pattern alone. With only five boroughs, the Pearson correlation (r = 0.829, p = 0.0829) is not statistically significant. Notably, Queens is the second most populous borough but ranks only fourth in reported crimes. To uncover clearer trends and more nuanced insights, a deeper analysis at the district level is necessary. This is why our interactive map focuses on community districts rather than boroughs.

Regarding who is targeted by these crimes, if you are a woman, you might be interested in knowing that 54% of the victims of crime in New York City are women. This is a significant statistic that highlights the importance of addressing violence against women in our communities. On the contrary, about 75% of the suspect are men.

Finally, looking at when crimes occur, we can see that most of the crimes are committed during the day, mostly between 12:00 and 18:00 with the pick around 15:00. This is likely due to the fact that more people are out during these hours, making it easier for criminals to commit crimes. However, it is important to note that this does not mean that it is safe to be out at night. In fact, the data shows that there are still a significant number of crimes committed at night, particularly between 18:00 and 24:00. It is always important to be aware of your surroundings and take precautions to stay safe, no matter what time of day it is.

Relation with Socio-Economic Factors

This leads us to a deeper question: why are crimes committed in the first place? It’s reasonable to think that many crimes are motivated by poverty, inequality, or a sense of injustice. When we look at the interactive map, it becomes clear that crime is not distributed evenly across districts—neither in terms of frequency nor in the types of crimes reported. It easy to think that socio-economic context of each district plays a role in shaping these patterns. For instance, wealthier districts might be more susceptible to theft or burglary, while economically disadvantaged areas could experience higher rates of drug- and weapon-related offenses. In the following section, we aim to uncover some of the socio-economic factors that influence crime rates across New York City.

The above table shows the top three districts in terms of crime counts between 2006 and 2023. Despite its relative affluence and high employment, Midtown Business District tops the crime charts, driven largely by opportunistic theft, while Jamaica/St. Albans, with moderate poverty and employment levels, sees slightly fewer incidents but a greater share of assaults. East New York, by contrast, suffers both high poverty and low employment, and although its total crime count ranks third, violent offenses (especially physical assault) account for the largest slice, underscoring how socio-economic stress shifts the balance from property crime toward interpersonal violence. The link between socio-economic factors and crime is complex, and at a first glance is not evident. However the living conditions in the district seem to have a direct impact on the types of crimes committed in each district. We will further investiguate this relationship.

Before diving into the crime data, let’s take a step back and examine the socio-economic landscape of New York City. The chart below illustrates the evolution of the poverty rate in NYC from 2006 to 2023. Over this period, the poverty rate has remained relatively stable, with a noticeable uptick in 2020, likely a consequence of the economic fallout from the COVID-19 pandemic. Despite minor fluctuations, poverty remains a pressing issue in the city: in 2023, 18.88% of New Yorkers were living below the federal poverty line, compared to the national average of 11.1%. This translates to nearly one in five residents struggling to make ends meet, with the threshold set at $14,580 annually for an individual and $30,000 for a family of four.[1]

Regarding employment, the employment rate in NYC has generally hovered between 55% and 60%, with a dip around 2020 that aligns with widespread job losses and economic instability caused by the pandemic. Interestingly, there appears to be a negative correlation between poverty and employment: as the employment rate decreases, the share of people living under the federal poverty line tends to rise. This is confirmed by a statistically significant Spearman correlation coefficient of ρ = –0.556 (p = 0.0254), supporting the intuitive link between employment and economic well-being. Unemployment remains a concern as well. In 2023, New York City's unemployment rate was 5.3%, considerably higher than the national average of 3.6%. This means that more than 1 in 20 individuals in the city’s labor force were unemployed, underlining persistent structural challenges in the urban job market.[2]

The table above highlights strong correlations between various crime categories and socioeconomic factors. Most crimes show a negative correlation with employment, suggesting that higher employment levels are generally associated with lower crime rates. This trend is particularly pronounced for offenses such as drug-related crimes, physical assault, and weapon possession. Notably, minor and major theft deviate from this pattern by showing positive correlations with employment. It’s important to note that the district associated with a crime refers to where the crime was committed, not necessarily where the perpetrator lives. One possible explanation is that areas with higher employment tend to be busier and more populated, providing more opportunities for thefts. These theft-related crimes also show negative correlations with poverty, which may reflect the greater availability of valuable items in wealthier districts. Among all categories, drug offenses display the most striking relationship: a strong positive correlation with poverty rate (+0.724) and a significant negative correlation with employment (-0.494). This suggests that drug-related crimes are far more prevalent in areas experiencing socioeconomic hardship, underscoring the link between precarity and this type of criminal behavior. Other crimes like intentional property fires also appear strongly tied to low employment levels. In contrast, crimes such as child abuse/endangerment, traffic violations, and fraud or forgery show no significant correlation with either socioeconomic factor, suggesting they occur relatively uniformly across the city, regardless of economic conditions.
